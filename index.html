What are the environmental consequences of computer device production?
Recourse Consumption
  • Inside digital devices are precious metals (like gold, silver, lithium, and palladium) and other rare earth metals. Key materials for computers, such as copper, aluminum, gold, cobalt, and rare earth elements, are extracted through mining.
  • This process often leads to deforestation, habitat destruction, and soil erosion, disrupting local ecosystems.
  • The physical footprint of mining can alter landscapes, making them uninhabitable for wildlife and affecting local communities’ land use.
Greenhouse Gases
  • Material processing and assembly consume vast amounts of energy, much of which still comes from fossil fuels, leading to greenhouse gas emissions.
  • The device and its parts may be manufactured in countries that rely heavily on fossil fuels (e.g., coal, oil, natural gas) to produce electricity.
  • Transporting the devices from distant countries also consumes a great deal of fossil fuels.
How does computer use harm the environment?
Contributes to climate change Creating, storing and moving this data requires electricity resulting in GHG emissions.

  • Burning fossil fuels is the main cause of greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions.
  • GHGs build up in the earth's atmosphere and contribute to climate change.
Energy Usage
  • The annual life cycle burden of a computer is 5,600 MJ (megajoules), however, only 34% of life cycle energy consumption occurs in the use phase. ﻿﻿﻿﻿
  • The rest of the energy is needed for the mining, manufacturing, packaging, and transportation processes required to make a computer.
  • The process of mining and refining these metals consumes natural resources and uses energy that produces GHG emissions.
  • Manufacturing the plastic casing and the glass screens also consumes both natural resources and electricity.
What are the negative effects of computer device disposal?
E-waste
  • Only a small fraction of digital devices are refurbished to extend their use or recycled for parts and the extraction of valuable minerals—most are sent to landfills.
  • Improper disposal can overwhelm landfills and waste management systems.
  • In some countries, children may serve as a source of cheap labour and their small hands give them an advantage in taking apart the smallest items.
  • These activities directly expose children to injury and high levels of hazardous substances.
  • Working as a waste picker is hazardous labour and is considered one of the worst forms of child labour by the ILO.
Health Risks
  • disposing of toxic materials without proper safety measures, leading to serious health issues.﻿﻿﻿﻿
  • e-waste can release harmful pollutants into the air, including dioxins and heavy metals, which can have serious health effects on nearby populations.
  • There are dangerous substances like lead, mercury, cadmium, and flame retardants in many computer parts. These chemicals have a chance to leak into groundwater and soil when these devices are disposed of incorrectly, threatening both the environment and human health.
